Galaxy Clusters

Galaxy clusters are the largest gravitational bound and virialized structures in the enitre Universe. Clusters emit in X-ray through the hot intracluster medium (ICM), which is a hot (tens of millions of degrees), diffuse, and optically thin plasma tracing the gravitational potential of the cluster. Also, signatures like shock fronts, sloshing cold fronts, and AGN feedback cavities allow us to trace the dynamic history of the cluster. At sub-mm wavelengths, the same ICM reshapes the cosmic microwave background through the Sunyaev–Zel’dovich (SZ) effect, giving a pressure-weighted view of the gas that’s beautifully complementary to the X-ray density-squared sensitivity. While X-rays let you map turbulence, metallicity, and thermodynamics on small scales, the sub-mm gives a mass proxy that stays robust with redshift, allowing you to trace cluster evolution into the early universe. Together, these wavelengths reveal clusters not as static piles of galaxies but as evolving laboratories of cosmic structure formation. By combining X-ray microphysics with SZ pressure maps, my work probes how clusters grow through mergers, regulate energy via AGN feedback, and ultimately serve as precision tools for cosmology where every shock, cavity, and pressure ridge encodes the history of structure assembly across cosmic time.
